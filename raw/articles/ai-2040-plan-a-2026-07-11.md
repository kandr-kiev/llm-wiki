Everyone wants to say that their own favorite policies will have great consequences and that the policies of their rivals will have terrible consequences. The first is people, 165 million of them. The second is AI agents: millions of copies spun up and shut down every hour, working around the clock at superhuman speeds.</p><p>Most of their work is slop. But enough of it is good that people are paying ten billion dollars a month for AIs that can, in theory at least, do anything on a computer that an employee can.</p><p>There is one job the AI companies want to automate more than any other—their own. Why exactly will AI never be able to do my job? What’s the barrier again?</p><p><strong>Congress is starting to pay more attention.</strong> They’ve long been hearing about AI: datacenters using too much water,<sup class="relative text-[var(--accent)] cursor-pointer" id="footnote-16"><a href="/footnotes#footnote-16" class="text-[var(--accent)] text-xs no-underline">16</a></sup> chatbots encouraging suicide, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/23/us/politics/nsa-lost-access-anthropic-tool.html" target="_blank">Mythos hacking NSA systems</a>—and of course, tech industry lobbyists warning that any whiff of regulation will make America immediately lose the race with China and spend the rest of history as a CCP tributary state.<sup class="relative text-[var(--accent)] cursor-pointer" id="footnote-17"><a href="/footnotes#footnote-17" class="text-[var(--accent)] text-xs no-underline">17</a></sup></p><p>Now they step back and ask: Where are we going with this?
