Even if it’s not affecting the actual answer, it’s still reminding you that it’s not a space where they’re not trying to sell you something. Someone is always trying to sell you something.&nbsp;\nThere’s already a lot of loss of trust right now in AI labs, if anyone trusted them to begin with. Right now, it’s at an all-time low. We’re seeing the rise of AI populism. We’re seeing lots of death threats against AI CEOs, as The Wall Street Journal covered the other day. There’s a lot of backlash, like you were saying about college kids booing AI at graduations. We’re seeing a huge backlash right now, and I just think it’s a strange time to be releasing a public-facing, “every consumer should buy this” AI hardware device.\nThat connects to a bigger issue for OpenAI in this Apple lawsuit, which is that the AI industry right now is basically built on unauthorized taking. I’m saying that instead of theft because there are a lot of lawsuits. I know our audience wants me to just call it theft and maybe it is just theft. And maybe we can just say that when we get to the end of the road of 10,000 different lawsuits. But that part of the AI industry feels like we have to studiously ignore it.\nThere’s a trillion-dollar question over whether training data is fair use that is as of yet unresolved. We can just go down the list of lawsuits where all of these companies have to defend themselves about training data. And if they lose any one of them, maybe this whole industry cost structure falls apart.\nThe inputs to AI right now are free. They just took the internet and they took all the books. We just saw, as a result of a hack, that Suno had to basically admit that it had scraped all of the music off of YouTube illegally. It just didn’t have permission, and I think YouTube feels a certain way about that and certainly the artists on YouTube feel a certain way about that.\nHere’s Apple saying OpenAI also took its hardware designs and its manufacturing know-how. It didn’t have permission for that either, those are Apple’s secrets. OpenAI hired people and they concocted this scheme to take all of that stuff without permission.&nbsp;\nAgain, culturally, this just feels like part of the AI industry to me, that they think they can just take everything to build super intelligence as fast as they can. And once they get there, all will be forgiven. But the public doesn’t seem to like it. Certainly, artists and writers and musicians don’t seem to like it, and now Apple doesn’t like it.\nIt feels like this lawsuit is going to be a proxy for that as much as anything else. Can you take all this stuff without permission? I’m not sure OpenAI has the cultural capital to fight back. What are they going to say?\nIt’s really ironic that this whole industry is only possible because of the amount of data it’s consumed, whether aboveboard or not. And AI labs, as well as just tech companies in general, get so litigious when others take that information elsewhere. So this whole thing is pretty ironic.&nbsp;\nThe AI industry is really famously cliquish. It’s almost incestuous, it’s like you work with the same people here, then you depart and go elsewhere. Then that same company buys your start-up that you created and then you leave that start-up to go somewhere else and you get acqui-hired by your old friend that worked at this other place. A lot of industries are like that, but this industry specifically is so much like that. And so yeah, I think it’s a bit ironic on both counts.&nbsp;\nPeople are going to leave Apple, and okay, they’re going to take some trade secrets. Was this incredibly egregious? Yes, some of these claims are crazy. But also, like you were mentioning, a lot of the reason that any of these companies are even successful in the first place is because of all the unauthorized taking they’ve done. It’s an interesting time to be watching all this play out.\nAll the AI companies get really mad when, I don’t know, Chinese AI companies distill their models. I’m just thinking about a jury and how Apple will play this case to a jury and be like, “You stole all the music, didn’t you? And then you also stole our manufacturing designs. And don’t you get mad when the Chinese companies distill ChatGPT?”\nIn the course of your reporting, does it ever occur to the people in the AI industry that being mad about distillation is the exact same feeling that artists and musicians have about all of their work being put into the model in the first place?\nI haven’t seen any of them even acknowledge that or at least even hint toward it one time, which is crazy. It just feels like there’s a disconnect there. They get so, so angry about distillation, they’re saying, “It’s against our terms of service!” They can’t even say it’s illegal, just against the terms of service. They write open letters to the government. They even come together with their mortal enemies and other AI labs to unite against distillation. So, it’s just interesting to me thatI’ve never seen that occur to them.\nI know a lot of people are probably listening to this and that they’re mad at me, and mad at us, for not making the claim that Apple is just filing this lawsuit because it was behind in AI. It missed with Apple Intelligence, it took a long time to catch back up, it’s only shipping it now. It had to go to Google and distill Google’s Gemini models and put them in its new Private Cloud Compute system, and all the stuff that it needed to do. But that is shipping now.\nDo you feel like the broader AI industry is just looking at Apple lashing out at being late? Because I have heard a lot of glimmers of that, that this is just Apple trying to slow down the biggest threat in consumer as it launches Apple Intelligence and tries to catch back up.\nThat could be definitely part of it. Everyone knows Apple has been famously incredibly behind in AI and it’s been a big topic of conversation and an embarrassment for the company for years. So, that could definitely be part of it.&nbsp;\nBut this lawsuit is not about someone “stealing trade secrets” for Siri. It’s about hardware, specifically “show and tell” of hardware, things like that. Hardware is something that Apple does amazingly and they are pretty much by all accounts the best at.\nSo, I think some people are thinking that. It’s a benefit for Apple if they’re able to kneecap a competitor from pulling ahead in any way. But the fact that this is a trade secrets lawsuit about hardware specifically I think has rolled back a few of those claims; it’s not like they’re trying to claim that OpenAI is stealing Siri AI’s secrets that it may not have many of.\n[Laughs] They’re like, “Please take everything we know about Siri AI. This is like the final battle. Actually, there are going to be a lot more battles, but this is a key battle in the longstanding AI arms race. This is the IPO year. Things are coming to a head. Investors are looking very closely at these companies in a way they haven’t before. They’re looking for them to turn a profit, or at least have a way to turn a profit in a way that they have not really paid too much attention to before, and it’s all coming down to the line. They cannot afford a distraction right now.&nbsp;\nThey are reshuffling, restructuring, changing executives around. When I’ve asked them about this, they will say, “Well, it’s all been one large plan that we’ve just been doing in little pieces. It’s not like we’ve pivoted a million times, we’ve always been going towards one thing and these are just different steps along that journey.” But it doesn’t always read that way to the general public.\nIn some ways, yes, Greg Brockman is taking control of product. That’s because Fidji Simo, the AGI chief, took leave because of her health and then she left permanently because of her health. So, it’s not all things that the company could control, but I do think there have been a lot of defections even from the rank-and-file staff. They’ve got to get more people. They’ve got to hire more, which will require even more money, and it’s not the time to get distracted by anything.\nIt’s worth noting that there’s some reporting saying OpenAI will delay its IPO. Especially because there’s no way we’re going to beat Anthropic to an IPO.” It seems like that might be a smart idea.\nIf you can put out a great hardware device and make some big splashy profitable entry into the consumer market, that’s a good story to tell in an IPO. Do you think OpenAI can close that gap? Because it has always been the company chasing the big consumer product vision, the big consumer market. If it can’t close the gap, it should just shut it down and compete with Anthropic for enterprise uses. I don’t know which one it’s going to be. Do you have a sense through all this reporting if OpenAI has realized how existential the consumer side of the debate is?\nI think they know to some extent, but not the full extent. tThey will get at it in speeches, in research papers, in tweets. They have some PR lines where they will acknowledge that this is a thing, but I just don’t think they realize how deeply this crevice goes. The gap is so wide. I don’t see them being the only company to close it — if it’s ever going to be closed. People are extremely worried about so many things when it comes to AI and they’re very emotional about it.&nbsp;\nIt’s not just logic. I think if it was just a logical gap — some people logically think it’s not good, some people logically think it is good — I think they could probably close that. But when emotions come into play, no, I don’t think that they will be able to fully bridge that gap.\nFor me, when I became an AI reporter six years ago, none of my friends really knew or cared what I wrote about. That was fine. It was really niche, whatever. Starting four years ago, it would come up at the bar, at the club. I’ll be walking down the street, someone’s asking me about it, and they don’t know I’m an AI reporter. So, when they learn I’m an AI reporter, it happens even more. But just randomly, this is coming up for people and making them extremely emotional. So yeah, I just don’t think OpenAI is going to be the company to bridge that gap.\nWe’ll see what happens with the hardware of it all. A lot of people I’ve been seeing — TikTok creators, creators on other platforms — are trying to change the narrative here, saying, “Hey, don’t get left behind. Learn how to use this stuff.” And it’s something that can be valid, but the way that they’re framing it a lot of the time is more, “Oh, it’s such a high learning curve. You must learn how to use it before it leaves you behind.”&nbsp;\nIt’s not that high of a learning curve, it depends on how you’re using it, but you know what I mean. Sometimes it is, yeah, if you’re using Codex and doing all sorts of intense vibe coding, yes, of course it is.
