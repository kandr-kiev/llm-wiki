How to enforce this is a separate question, of course, but the rule exists.<p>We don&#x27;t have a similar rule yet about article content but my sense is that the community mostly doesn&#x27;t want to read it—or, to put it a bit more conservatively, sharply discounts it. This is why we see so many &quot;just show me the prompt&quot; responses, along with others like this: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;news.ycombinator.com&#x2F;genai-pushback">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;news.ycombinator.com&#x2F;genai-pushback</a>. I built that list so I have something to send to users who email about why their genai articles got flagged.<p>A fascinating arms race is happening: the AIs are training on the humans but the human hivemind is also training on the AIs. Readers are developing allergic sensitivities to language that sounds like an LLM produced it. The AIs will adapt to this over time, but humans will adapt in turn. Where it ends up is anyone&#x27;s guess. I have an optimistic view, but I&#x27;ve already been wrong about this so many times that I have low confidence in it.<p>The near-term picture is that there is a class distinction between writing (and writers) that use genai vs. writing that does not. That is, as soon as the allergic &quot;this sounds like an LLM&quot; reaction kicks in, the writing immediately gets relegated to the low-status bucket in the reader&#x27;s mind. That doesn&#x27;t mean it won&#x27;t still get looked at - but it is now under a stigma.<p>(I was rather pleased with the originality of this observation and then I remembered pg had written about &quot;writes and write-nots&quot; in <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;paulgraham.com&#x2F;writes.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;paulgraham.com&#x2F;writes.html</a>.
