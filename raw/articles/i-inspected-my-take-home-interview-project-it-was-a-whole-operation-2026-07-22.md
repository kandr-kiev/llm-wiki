text-zinc-100">I Inspected My Take-Home Interview Project. It Was a Whole Operation.</h1><time dateTime="July 23, 2026" class="order-first flex items-center text-base text-zinc-500"><span class="h-4 w-0.5 rounded-full bg-zinc-500"></span><span class="ml-3">July 23, 2026</span></time></header><div class="markdown-body mt-8 prose prose-invert" data-mdx-content="true"> <p>Discuss on <a href="https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49013036" target="_blank">Hacker News</a> or <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7485790360367980544/" target="_blank">LinkedIn</a>.</p>
<p>A recruiter slid into my LinkedIn DMs last Thursday with a Python developer role. I was thrilled that someone had reached out directly, so I asked for more details. When he shared the role description, company name, and the estimated pay, I figured I had nothing to lose.</p>
<p>Here is the initial message:</p>
<div align="center"><p><img src="/_astro/Screenshot_20260719-211017.DYnt8aCJ_Z1UUdvu.webp" alt="Intro message" loading="lazy" decoding="async" fetchpriority="auto" width="846" height="1617"></p></div>
<p>Offering $10,000-$15,000 a month for a remote-first, contract-to-hire role is just too good.
Also, why is this guy revealing pay info before even we met? I thought recruiters play the “you first, me next” game. Rookie mistake.</p>
<div align="center"><p><img src="/_astro/Screenshot_20260719-211026.DcWSSXgd_1u3gxU.webp" alt="Pay" loading="lazy" decoding="async" fetchpriority="auto" width="841" height="522"></p></div>
<p>Red flags immediately started waving. Why the huge budget? (Well, huge by Indian standards for a remote role; not exactly outrageous by US standards, but good enough to raise an eyebrow.) I looked up the company and saw it was a Y Combinator startup. YC companies aren’t exactly known for conventional operations, so it wasn’t completely outside the realm of possibility. Still, if a company has that kind of cash to throw around, they usually have a much more structured hiring pipeline. I decided to proceed, but kept my guard up.</p>
<p>I sent over my resume. The recruiter quickly approved it and handed over a take-home assignment via a Google Drive link containing a zip archive and a PDF with instructions. Here’s the original drive link: <a href="https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/18i1KDFXAPv7lqfBGddxj7IOnDy8J6VeH?usp=drive_link">https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/18i1KDFXAPv7lqfBGddxj7IOnDy8J6VeH?usp=drive_link</a>. I made my copy here in case they delete theirs: <a href="https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1DZYWezjpwolsxXnM5F04_Ng3nzTRYpVS?usp=sharing">https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1DZYWezjpwolsxXnM5F04_Ng3nzTRYpVS?usp=sharing</a>.</p>
<p>Assessment PDF was surpringly legitimate looking. It’s about how to improve the existing codebase, architectural suggestions, some git operations etc..</p>
<p>I extracted the zip. At first glance, it was just a boilerplate FastAPI backend using SQLAlchemy; pretty standard stuff. I checked <code>requirements.txt</code> for any obvious typosquatting or malicious packages, but it was completely clean. For a brief second, I thought my suspicions were unfounded and this was a legitimate opportunity.</p>
<p>This is just a habit (may be from doing CTFs), whenever I get a random project folder, I just run <code>tree -a</code> to see what’s lurking in the hidden directories. But this might be the first time it paid off in the real world.</p>

<p>This second stage does a lot of heavy lifting. It quietly installs Node.js, configures the system path, downloads a <code>package.json</code> and a <code>parser.js</code> file, installs the required dependencies, and runs the parser invisibly.</p>
<p>I took a look at <code>parser.js</code>. The code was heavily obfuscated, a complete mess to read manually. Remember the <code>id</code> parameter? I tried changing it in my request and received a completely different script back. The attackers are likely assigning unique identifiers to track individual candidates, serving customized payloads to each victim.</p>
<p>Since <code>parser.js</code> was a brick wall, I pivoted to <code>package.json</code>. Unlike the parser, this has to be standard JSON for <code>npm</code> to process it.</p>

If they were dropping a Bitcoin miner, distributing specific hashing tasks to unique IDs would make sense. But Ethereum shifted away from Proof of Work; it doesn’t rely on mining anymore. They are likely using Hardhat to locate and drain crypto wallets or interact with local browser extensions? idk.</p>

<h2 id="the-scam-goes-deeper">The Scam goes deeper</h2>
<p>After realizing this was a widespread campaign, I did a bit more digging and found that people are getting different variations of this attack. Some folks received a zip file containing a <code>.vscode</code> folder. Inside, the attackers hid commands configured to run as soon as the directory is opened in VSCode (launch commands).</p>
<p>Pretty clever.</p>
<p>You don’t even have to run a git command, just opening this directory in VSCode is enough to get infected.</p>
<p>Also, it’s pretty evident now that this has nothing to do with Zavopay. The attackers just used whatever company name they found to make the offer look legitimate. Out of curiosity, I ran git log to inspect the project’s commit history, wondering if they left any custom traces. It turns out, they just cloned a random public repository.</p>

<p>A quick search led me straight to the original repo: <a href="https://github.com/Bgogoi123/personal-finance-service">https://github.com/Bgogoi123/personal-finance-service</a>. They literally just took someone’s innocent FastAPI project and slapped a malicious hidden directory on top of it.</p>
<p>Naturally, the next move was pivoting from defense to offense. I wanted to see if the attackers left any vulnerable services exposed on their IP.</p>
<p>An Nmap scan revealed three open ports. Two of them were unresponsive to version detection. Port 22 was running OpenSSH 9.6p1 on Ubuntu. Since that version was released just over a week prior to this scan, there were no known CVEs I could leverage to poke around their infrastructure.</p>

<p>So, they had decent OPSEC on their server, even if their malware deployment was a bit loud. That’s where the trail goes cold for now. Stay safe out there, and always check those hidden directories before running someone else’s code.</p>
