  <section class="section-markdown">
      <p><strong>Open Book Touch</strong> is the device I&#8217;ve been trying to build for six years: a small, beautiful, completely open source e-book reader that does one thing and does it well. There are no physical buttons on the front; the device is a single, perfectly symmetrical 4.26-inch front-lit e-paper touchscreen, one centimeter thin in its enclosure. Open Book Touch slips into a pocket and disappears until you unlock it to read.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s taken a while to get here, but the TL;DR is this: <em>it&#8217;s real now</em>. Earlier Open Books could show you a wall of plain text. This one shows your book covers in a gorgeous, deeply designed interface, reads EPUB files (<em>finally!</em>), and renders dozens of writing systems. There&#8217;s Wi-Fi too, but mostly for getting books on, not for living online. And the whole thing is open source, both hardware and software, so you can hack it, fork it, tear it down, and build it back up.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s a liberated book for the people.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/756c/e8be06e7-c723-42cd-9591-a6cda9bc756c_gallery-lg.jpg"

  </div>
</div>
<p>Open Book Touch isn&#8217;t trying to be a tablet. Like all of the objects I design, it makes a deliberate set of tradeoffs to do its one job beautifully:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>It's for reading, not for everything.</strong> No notifications, no browser, no feed to scroll. The Wi-Fi is there to sync the time and download books, nothing more. There's a soft keyboard for simple tasks, but it's not meant for note-taking or vibe coding.</li>
<li><strong>It's a microcontroller, not a Linux box.</strong> As it turns out, less can be more: Open Book Touch boots straight into the book you're reading, sips power at under a milliampere, and runs readable C++ firmware (on ESP-IDF/FreeRTOS) that you can understand and hack on.</li>
<li><strong>It's small, not big.</strong> It's small enough to forget you're carrying it in a jacket pocket, and light enough to fit in an ultralight backpacker's kit. Open Book Touch is more &quot;mass market paperback&quot; than &quot;leather-bound hardcover&quot; — but it'll go places a 10-inch tablet won't.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="spoilers-its-a-book">Spoilers: It&#8217;s a Book</h2><p>The whole point of an e-reader is the reading, so that&#8217;s where most of the work has gone. Open Book Touch reads <strong>EPUB</strong> and <strong>plain text</strong> files straight off its microSD card: drop in your books, and they show up on the shelf.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/1bb6/834a7347-b835-4c6a-b975-af0797f11bb6_gallery-lg.jpg"

    </a>

  </div>
</div>
<p>But &quot;it shows text&quot; is table stakes. Open Book Touch implements a real typesetting engine, alongside gorgeous fonts in multiple weights and sizes to truly up the ante:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Proper typography.</strong> Lines are justified with even word spacing, words hyphenate at the right places, and pages break cleanly (hyphenation dictionaries available for English, Spanish, French, and Italian). Images embedded in a book render inline, 1-bit dithered to look crisp on e-paper.</li>
<li><strong>Fonts that were actually drawn.</strong> Your books are set in carefully drawn bitmap versions of <strong>Lucida Bright</strong> and <strong>Lucida Sans</strong>, in three sizes, each with true <strong>bold</strong> and <em>italic</em> letterforms drawn at every weight. (These were open-sourced in 1989 by Sun Microsystems; we are the inheritors of a proud legacy).</li>
<li><strong>The stuff you reach for without thinking.</strong> Tap and hold to highlight a passage or look up a word. Dog-ear a page. Sort your books onto shelves. Put the device down for a week, pick it up, and it opens right back to the page — and the spot on the page — where you left off.</li>
</ul>

<p>It&#8217;s a reader you&#8217;ll actually want to read on.</p>
<h2 id="light-reading">Light Reading</h2><p>Our e-paper panel packs the 480 × 800 pixels you&#8217;d normally find on a much larger 7.5-inch display into just 4.26 inches diagonal, so everything is remarkably sharp. It&#8217;s a 1-bit display at heart (crisp black on white), and it&#8217;s dense enough that even your book covers, dithered to pure black-and-white, look genuinely delightful on the home screen.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/f194/38422b6c-cec5-49c0-8750-0143958cf194_gallery-lg.jpg"

    </a>

  </div>
</div>
<p>The best part, though, is the frontlight. With <strong>both warm and cool LEDs</strong> in the frontlight module, you&#8217;re not stuck with one harsh color temperature: you can warm it all the way down for reading in bed, cool it for daylight, or dial in something in between, with fully adjustable brightness. You can read all night without wrecking your night vision (or keeping your partner awake with a reading light).</p>
<p>E-paper is still e-paper. The screen refreshes at a readerly pace; you&#8217;re not going to play DOOM on it. The fast 1-bit mode drives everything you touch, page turns included. The richer 2-bit grayscale takes several seconds to draw, so we save it for the lock screen: your current book cover, or a photo of your own.</p>

<h2 id="read-the-world">Read the World</h2><p>This whole project started with a font. Back in 2019, I stumbled across GNU Unifont: a bitmap font with a glyph for nearly every character in the Unicode standard. I wanted to build a device that could put <em>all the languages of the world</em> in your hand.</p>
<p>Open Book Touch is the realization of that idea.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/9915/ca2c255f-9b4a-4ae4-a4ad-65dbde749915_gallery-lg.jpg"
      data-thumbnail-path="/img/9915/ca2c255f-9b4a-4ae4-a4ad-65dbde749915_gallery-thumb.jpg">
      <img alt="" src="/img/9915/ca2c255f-9b4a-4ae4-a4ad-65dbde749915_md-xl.jpg" width="1600" />
    </a>

  </div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><strong>Unifont ships on the device</strong> as a universal fallback covering roughly 70,000 glyphs spanning the world's writing systems. Whatever language your book is in, it's likelier than not to render legibly.</li>
<li><strong>The interface itself is localized</strong> into English, Spanish, French, German, Japanese, Arabic, and Hebrew, with more languages to come.</li>
<li><strong>Right-to-left and Arabic scripts just work</strong>: We've implemented the Unicode bidirectional algorithm, and letter shaping for Perso-Arabic languages, so letters connect and change shape as they should.</li>
</ul>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/0520/45e7524b-778c-4e59-b323-743670e20520_gallery-lg.jpg"

    </a>

  </div>
</div>
<p>Latin scripts look great today in Lucida. Cyrillic scripts like Russian and Ukrainian are supported via GNU Unifont, along with Arabic and Hebrew. Unifont also powers our Chinese and Japanese support. Complex Indic scripts don&#8217;t yet shape fully, but as an open source project, we&#8217;d welcome your pull requests!</p>
<h2 id="an-open-focus">An Open Focus</h2><p>Open Book Touch is open hardware with MIT-licensed firmware — schematics, board files, enclosure CAD, and source, all of it out in the open. But to make this real, I had to build something bigger than the firmware. <strong>Focus</strong>, a new, bespoke C++ application framework in the spirit of NeXTSTEP&#8217;s AppKit and Apple&#8217;s UIKit, comes complete with view controllers, a touch-and-gesture system, a full set of on-screen widgets, and even an on-screen keyboard.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a
      class="js-carousel-image "
      href="/img/c528/9ba5b526-a4f3-4023-be3c-57b1a159c528_gallery-lg.jpg"
      data-thumbnail-path="/img/c528/9ba5b526-a4f3-4023-be3c-57b1a159c528_gallery-thumb.jpg">
      <img alt="" src="/img/c528/9ba5b526-a4f3-4023-be3c-57b1a159c528_md-xl.jpg" width="1600" />
    </a>

  </div>
</div>
<p><strong>Focus</strong> is the system I built to power Open Book, and yet it isn&#8217;t limited to Open Book. Focus is platform-agnostic by design: the screen sits behind a small, swappable driver, and the user interface supports touch and button-based input, so the same view controllers, layout, text, and interactions run whether you&#8217;re drawing to a big 480 × 800 touchscreen or a tiny monochrome LCD with a joystick.</p>
<p>You&#8217;ll hear more about Focus in an upcoming backer update, but for the moment, I&#8217;m stoked to announce that <a href="https://github.com/joeycastillo/focus">we&#8217;re open-sourcing Focus today</a>. A framework like Focus is only worth something when it gets used, and I want it out in the world for folks to start to hack on. As for the rest — the board files, the enclosure CAD, and the source code itself — <strong>we plan to release all of it when we ship.</strong> The files go public as units reach the backers who funded them, so the people who made this possible get their devices before anyone can spin up a clone.</p>
<h2 id="whats-inside">What&#8217;s Inside</h2><p>At the center is an <strong>ESP32-S3</strong>, a dual-core microcontroller with Wi-Fi and Bluetooth LE, and we&#8217;ve given it room to work:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>16 MB of flash</strong> for firmware with room to spare for custom fonts and additional apps</li>
<li><strong>8 MB of fast PSRAM</strong> for memory-hungry jobs like parsing EPUB files</li>
<li>A <strong>microSD slot</strong> for your library</li>
<li>A <strong>32.768 kHz crystal</strong> for timekeeping (useful for remembering when you highlighted that passage)</li>
<li><strong>USB Type-C</strong> with integrated LiPo charging and charge monitoring</li>
</ul>
<p>We finally said goodbye to fragile Arduino workflows and built the firmware on <strong>ESP-IDF and FreeRTOS</strong>, with <strong>SQLite</strong> quietly managing your library&#8217;s metadata under the hood. When your Open Book is on the same Wi-Fi network, you can <strong>send books to it from a web browser</strong>, no cables required.</p>



<div class="image-single image-single-normal">
  <div class="image-single-inner image-single-inner-h">
      
    <a

      href="/img/ade3/73676ef6-9e9b-4b19-8016-10047939ade3_gallery-lg.jpg"

<p>And of course, you can still write code for it using Arduino or CircuitPython if you&#8217;d rather treat it as an absurdly nice e-paper dev board; it&#8217;s been tested with both.</p>
<p>And because so much of the hard-won wisdom from <a href="https://www.crowdsupply.com/oddly-specific-objects/sensor-watch">Sensor Watch</a> went into this, it&#8217;s frugal with power: the ESP32-S3&#8217;s low-power RISC-V coprocessor sits and watches for your touch while the main chip sleeps, and when the device is locked, only the ultra-low-power real-time-clock continues to tick. In our testing, you can expect about a week of reading with the frontlight off, and well over a month of standby. Still, of course, the more you crank the frontlight, the more you&#8217;ll be reaching for that USB-C cable.</p>
<p>We&#8217;re committing to an 800 mAh battery as our floor, but because the case has room for the 1200 mAh cell we&#8217;ve been testing, we&#8217;ll ship the largest one we can reliably source. If we can manage that, reading time goes well past that one-week baseline.</p>
<h2 id="make-it-your-own">Make It Your Own</h2><p>Open Book Touch is yours in a way most devices aren&#8217;t. The battery is one you can swap yourself. The library is one you load yourself. Nothing phones home, and nobody can reach in to delete a book you own. The device answers to you, and no one else.</p>
<p>That goes for how it looks and feels, too. The case we&#8217;re planning to ship (and the one shown on this page) is 3D-printed: slim, lightweight, and engineered to tight tolerances. If the campaign draws enough interest, moving to an injection-molded enclosure is a stretch goal — a real step up in fit and finish — but the 3D-printed shell is what we&#8217;re committing to today.</p>

</div>
<p>Here&#8217;s the part I&#8217;m most excited about though: that case? It&#8217;s snap fit, which means it&#8217;s easy to take apart and put back together. And because it was designed to be 3D-printed to begin with, the files to make your own ship right alongside the rest of our CAD. Want one in brown like a leather-bound book? Sparkly pink? Translucent purple, like the old Game Boy Color? Go for it! I want Open Book Touch to look like <em>your</em> book, not mine.</p>
<p>For the folks who&#8217;d rather buy nice than print their own, we&#8217;re also offering a <strong>special edition</strong>: the same Open Book Touch, dressed up in premium materials and finish, inspired by Bunnie&#8217;s &quot;omakase&quot; Precursor. The exact form and materials are still taking shape, but I think it&#8217;s going to be something really special that you&#8217;re not going to want to miss.</p>
<h2 id="features-specifications">Features &amp; Specifications</h2><ul>
<li><strong>Processor:</strong> ESP32-S3 dual-core MCU with Wi-Fi and Bluetooth LE</li>
<li><strong>Program memory:</strong> 16 MB quad SPI flash</li>
<li><strong>RAM:</strong> 8 MB octal SPI PSRAM</li>
<li><strong>Display:</strong> 4.26-inch, 480 × 800 e-paper; 1-bit and 2-bit grayscale with dithering</li>
<li><strong>Frontlight:</strong> five warm + five cool LEDs, independently dimmable</li>
<li><strong>Touchscreen:</strong> capacitive, FT6336, low-power with interrupt capability</li>
<li><strong>Storage:</strong> microSD card slot</li>
<li><strong>Battery:</strong> minimum 800 mAh user-replaceable LiPo with integrated charging and monitoring</li>
<li><strong>Connector:</strong> USB Type-C</li>
<li><strong>Enclosure:</strong> 3D-printed plastic; snap-fit, with 3D-printable CAD for custom shells (injection molding a stretch goal)</li>
<li><strong>Reads:</strong> EPUB and plain text, with hyphenation, justification, and inline images</li>
<li><strong>Languages:</strong> ~70,000-glyph Unifont fallback; UI localized in seven languages including right-to-left</li>
<li><strong>Open source:</strong> MIT-licensed firmware; open hardware (released at shipping)</li>
<li><strong>Dimensions:</strong> 78 × 120 × 10 mm</li>
<li><strong>Weight:</strong> Approximately 3 oz (85 grams)</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="manufacturing-plan">Manufacturing Plan</h2><p>Open Book Touch is being manufactured with the help of <a href="https://www.crowdsupply.com/nextpcb/launchpad">NextPCB&#8217;s Launchpad program</a>, which handles PCB assembly and helps us get from a working prototype to a proper product. The front-lit, touch-enabled e-paper panel — the heart of the device — comes from Good Display, our display partner, which has been making this specific kind of display for exactly this kind of product. The enclosure is 3D-printed, tuned for a clean fit and finish. We&#8217;ve also priced out the move to injection molding for an even nicer shell, and it&#8217;s totally doable if the campaign draws enough interest.</p>
<h2 id="fulfillment-logistics">Fulfillment &amp; Logistics</h2><p>Once the campaign succeeds, your Open Book Touch will be produced, shipped to Crowd Supply&#8217;s warehouse, and delivered to backers worldwide through Crowd Supply&#8217;s fulfillment partner, Mouser. We&#8217;re targeting <strong>early 2027</strong> to get the first units into backers&#8217; hands. That first batch is sized to the number of displays we can count on; additional units ship in waves after it, first-come, first-served in the order pledges arrive.</p>
<p>The whole schedule leans on the display; that&#8217;s where we need some real talk.</p>
<h2 id="risks-challenges">Risks &amp; Challenges</h2><p>The big risk first: Open Book Touch is built around a specific e-paper panel with frontlight and touch screen from Good Display. Alas: the global supply of e-paper driver chips is tight right now, and lead times for some of these ICs are stretched to eight months at a minimum.</p>
<p>Good Display has told us they&#8217;ll try to set aside about 1,000 units of their next production run for us, but they can&#8217;t guarantee that until we place the order. So the moment this campaign hits its goal, I&#8217;m placing that order and fronting the cost myself, before the campaign money has even cleared. That pulls the part with the scariest lead time off the critical path while we get to work on the PCBA and the enclosure.</p>
<p>The display is the one risk I can actually do something about. The rest are the ones nobody can — and quoting you a device in early 2027 means standing behind a six-to-nine-month window in which a great deal of the world can happen:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The wider parts squeeze.</strong> The same demand that stretched our display-driver lead times is also stretching the MicroSD card market, and could affect anything else with little warning.</li>
<li><strong>Tariffs and trade.</strong> Open Book Touch is built from parts made in East Asia and imported into the U.S.; this is a situation that changes week by week, and sometimes day by day.</li>
<li><strong>Disruption in East Asia.</strong> The screen, the chip, the board, and the tooling all come from one part of the world. A regional conflict or natural disaster could impact our ability to deliver.</li>
<li><strong>Instability closer to home.</strong> This window runs through both the U.S. Semiquincentennial and the November midterm elections. That's a lot of national <em>vibes</em> for a U.S.-based creator and distributor.</li>
</ul>
<p>Barring any larger calamities, we&#8217;re sizing the first batch around the screens we can genuinely count on, and shipping the rest on a first-come, first-served basis as more panels come off the line. My promise: the blows I can absorb, I will; I&#8217;ve built in margin, and even at break-even, the campaign units will ship. The risks nobody can absorb, I&#8217;ll meet the only way I honestly can: I won&#8217;t quote a ship date I don&#8217;t believe in, and if the timeline slips, you&#8217;ll hear it from me the moment I know.</p>
