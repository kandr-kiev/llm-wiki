                <p>Look at the analytics setup of almost any Laravel SaaS and you'll find the same thing: three tools, three logins, three versions of the truth.</p>
<p>There's a <strong>traffic tool</strong> (Google Analytics, or Plausible or Fathom if the team cares about privacy) that counts visitors and sessions. There's a <strong>subscription-analytics tool</strong> (ChartMogul, Baremetrics, ProfitWell) bolted onto Stripe to turn charges into MRR and churn. And there's an <strong>ad pixel</strong> (the Meta or Google tag) dropped into the frontend so the ad platforms can attribute conversions.</p>
<p>Each one does its job. The problem is what happens between them: nothing. They don't share data, they don't share a definition of a &quot;customer&quot;, and not one of them can answer the question that actually decides where next month's budget goes.</p>
<p>This post is the case for collapsing that stack into one, server-side, built for Laravel. It's what we built <a href="https://simplestats.io/?utm_source=laravelnews&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_campaign=newsletter_2026_07">SimpleStats</a> for. Not because three tabs is one too many, but because the value was hiding in the gaps between them all along.</p>
<hr />
<h2><a id="content-the-three-tools-youre-duct-taping-together" href="#content-the-three-tools-youre-duct-taping-together" class="not-prose mr-1 text-gray-600 font-normal heading-permalink" aria-hidden="true" title="Permalink">#</a>The Three Tools You're Duct-Taping Together</h2>
<p>It's worth being precise about what each layer actually knows, because the blind spots are the whole story.</p>
<p><strong>The traffic layer</strong> (Fathom, Plausible, GA) knows where people come from and what they look at. It sees <code>utm_campaign</code>, referrers, pageviews. What it deliberately does not see is money. Fathom and Plausible don't touch revenue by design; GA sees, at best, a client-side conversion event, and 30 to 50% of even that is eaten by ad blockers before it arrives.</p>
<p><strong>The revenue layer</strong> (ChartMogul, Baremetrics, ProfitWell) knows money in exquisite detail. Every charge, every upgrade, every cancellation, turned into MRR, churn and LTV. What it has never heard of is a marketing channel. Stripe doesn't know your <code>utm_campaign</code>, so nothing built on top of Stripe does either.</p>
<p><strong>The attribution layer</strong> (the Meta / Google pixel) tries to bridge the two, but only for the platform's own benefit and only client-side. It fires when someone converts, reports back to the ad platform, and gets blocked by the same ad blockers as everything else. It optimizes ad delivery. It does not give <em>you</em> a clean, honest table of profit per channel.</p>
<p>So you end up with three dashboards, each holding one third of the picture, and a quarterly spreadsheet where someone tries to staple them together by hand.</p>
<hr />
<h2><a id="content-why-three-tools-is-worse-than-the-sum" href="#content-why-three-tools-is-worse-than-the-sum" class="not-prose mr-1 text-gray-600 font-normal heading-permalink" aria-hidden="true" title="Permalink">#</a>Why Three Tools Is Worse Than the Sum</h2>
<p>The instinct is to think &quot;three tools, three jobs, fine.&quot; But the numbers that actually run a subscription business live <em>between</em> the tools, not inside any one of them:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Which campaign brings customers who stay?</strong> Needs traffic (channel) and revenue (retention). No single tool has both.</li>
<li><strong>What is a customer from the newsletter actually worth?</strong> Needs channel attribution and LTV. Split across two tools that don't share an ID.</li>
<li><strong>Is this campaign profitable after ad spend and churn?</strong> Needs all three layers at once.</li>
</ul>
<p>Every one of those requires joining data across tools that were never designed to be joined. And the join is brutal: the traffic tool identifies people by a client-side cookie, the revenue tool by a Stripe customer ID, the pixel by its own anonymous ID. Reconciling them is manual, lossy, and stale the moment it's done.</p>
<p>The result is a stack that can tell you <em>how much</em> traffic and <em>how much</em> revenue, but never reliably connects a specific euro of revenue back to the specific campaign that earned it. That connection is the single most valuable thing an analytics stack can give a SaaS, and the three-tool setup is structurally incapable of it.</p>
<hr />
<h2><a id="content-the-one-thing-that-ties-it-together" href="#content-the-one-thing-that-ties-it-together" class="not-prose mr-1 text-gray-600 font-normal heading-permalink" aria-hidden="true" title="Permalink">#</a>The One Thing That Ties It Together</h2>
<p><a href="https://simplestats.io/?utm_source=laravelnews&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_campaign=newsletter_2026_07">SimpleStats</a> takes a different starting point. Instead of three tools each watching one slice from the outside, it tracks the whole customer journey as <strong>one connected chain, server-side, from inside your Laravel app</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>visitor → registration → login → payment</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Because all four are captured in the same place and linked to the same visitor, every downstream number inherits the acquisition context for free. A payment isn't just a payment, it's a payment from the visitor who arrived via <code>utm_campaign=spring-sale</code> from Germany six weeks ago. Nothing has to be reconciled after the fact, because it was never separate to begin with.</p>
<p>And because it happens on the server, there's no pixel, no cookie banner for the analytics, and no ad-blocker hole. The data is complete, which is the quiet prerequisite for every other number being trustworthy.</p>
<p>That single design choice is what lets one tool do the job of three, without the gaps.</p>

<h2><a id="content-the-same-stack-in-one-place" href="#content-the-same-stack-in-one-place" class="not-prose mr-1 text-gray-600 font-normal heading-permalink" aria-hidden="true" title="Permalink">#</a>The Same Stack, in One Place</h2>

<p>Unique visitors, sources, campaigns, countries, devices, conversion rate. The privacy-first web analytics you'd expect, GDPR-compliant and unaffected by ad blockers because it's server-side. This is the base of the funnel, and every metric above it can be sliced by these same dimensions.</p>

<p>MRR and ARR, MRR movements (new, expansion, contraction, churn, reactivation), Net and Gross Revenue Retention, Quick Ratio, revenue churn and LTV. The full subscription dashboard you'd otherwise pay a separate tool for. The difference: every one of these can be filtered by the channel the customers came from. We go deep on this in <a href="https://simplestats.io/blog/saas-metrics-for-laravel-mrr-churn-ltv">SaaS Metrics for Laravel</a>.</p>

<p>Enter your ad spend per campaign and you get ROAS, CAC, net profit, LTV:CAC and CAC-payback per channel, computed from revenue that's <em>already</em> attributed to the campaign that earned it. This is what the pixel was always supposed to give you and never quite did. The full breakdown is in <a href="https://simplestats.io/blog/marketing-roi-for-laravel">Marketing ROI for Laravel</a>.</p>

More in <a href="https://simplestats.io/blog/laravel-retention-analytics">Laravel Retention Analytics</a>.</p>

<h2><a id="content-the-question-the-stack-can-finally-answer" href="#content-the-question-the-stack-can-finally-answer" class="not-prose mr-1 text-gray-600 font-normal heading-permalink" aria-hidden="true" title="Permalink">#</a>The Question the Stack Can Finally Answer</h2>
<p>Put those layers on top of the same connected chain and a question that used to need a quarter-end spreadsheet becomes a filter click:</p>
<p><em>Of the customers our newsletter brought in, what's their MRR, how many are still active after 90 days, and did the campaign turn a profit after ad spend?</em></p>
<p>Traffic tool alone can't answer it. Revenue tool alone can't. The pixel definitely can't. The combined stack can, because it was one dataset the whole time.</p>
<p>That's the real pitch. Not &quot;three tools in one&quot; as a bundle, but three tools in one as a <em>join</em> that the separate tools can never perform. A filter click instead of a spreadsheet.</p>
<p>Or, as of recently: literally a question you type.</p>

One connected dataset is the quiet prerequisite for AI on your analytics being more than a demo.</p>
