href="/mcp-is-a-fad/">MCP is a Fad</a></li></ul></div></nav></aside><main class="col col--7"><article class=""><header><h1 class="title_f1Hy">The Arguments Against Open Source AI are Very Bad</h1><div class="container_mt6G margin-vert--md"><time datetime="2026-07-23T00:00:00.000Z">July 23, 2026</time> · <!-- -->8 min read</div></header><div id="__blog-post-container" class="markdown"><p>The release of Kimi K3 has opened a fresh round of angst and confused discourse. There&#x27;s a loud cohort of journalists, business leaders, and politicians arguing that open source AI is a dangerous threat. OpenAI&#x27;s <a href="https://x.com/deanwball/status/2078133895766114412" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Dean Ball</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>One probable outcome of an open-weight-model-dominant world is full AI communism... rather than a market product, AI is a &quot;public good&quot;</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Freely available AI for anyone? The horror!</p>
<p>Frontier labs&#x27; case against open source AI is essentially: Open source models<sup><a href="#user-content-fn-1-e066d4" id="user-content-fnref-1-e066d4" data-footnote-ref="true" aria-describedby="footnote-label" class="footnoteRefStickyNavbar_i6ta">1</a></sup> are dangerous (<em>and un-American!</em>). We should open the AI Pandora&#x27;s Box, but only with responsible gatekeepers (<em>toll collectors, preferably us!</em>). Only trusted users (<em>our most profitable customers</em>) should be able to use it.</p>
<p>I want to address some bad arguments against open source AI, but some corrections on how the argument is being framed are in order:</p>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="open-source-software-is-the-foundation-for-commercial-software">Open source software is the foundation for commercial software<a href="#open-source-software-is-the-foundation-for-commercial-software" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to Open source software is the foundation for commercial software" title="Direct link to Open source software is the foundation for commercial software" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>Ball&#x27;s framing strolls past the fact that <em>open source software is the foundation of all proprietary software.</em> This includes frontier models, which at the end of the day are software products.</p>
<p>Open source software is counterintuitive to people outside of the software industry. Why work hard on a product, and give it away for free?</p>
<p>A software program is a stack of programs, with each layer built on top of another. To build Uber, you need programming language frameworks, software to send and receive web traffic, data analysis tools, and countless other components. Most of these are not differentiators for a commercial enterprise, so it serves commercial actors to cooperate on lower components in the stack and compete on the higher level pieces that actually differentiate their products.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" alt="software stack" src="/assets/images/stack-66e4fd377352ecc706f357355068ebdf.png" width="967" height="472" class="img_ev3q"></p>
<p>Frontier labs would <em>very much</em> like AI models to <em>not</em> fall into the category of &quot;so commonplace that it doesn&#x27;t make sense to compete on&quot;. Whether that happens remains to be seen.</p>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="open-source-software-is-very-difficult-to-suppress">Open source software is very difficult to suppress<a href="#open-source-software-is-very-difficult-to-suppress" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to Open source software is very difficult to suppress" title="Direct link to Open source software is very difficult to suppress" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>In reality, the argument about suppressing open source models is mostly beside the point. History tells us that suppression of open source software is <em>extremely</em> difficult, and attempting to do so only serves to weaken companies against international competitors. A brief history of encryption is illustrative:</p>
<p>Today, <strong>PGP</strong> is a commonplace tool anyone can use, and most devs are at least familiar with. But when Phil Zimmermann invented it in 1991, the U.S. government <a href="https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/08/us-export-controls-and-published-encryption-source-code-explained" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">considered encryption to be military technology</a>. A <a href="https://philzimmermann.com/EN/essays/BookPreface.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">criminal investigation was opened</a> against Zimmermann.</p>
<p>When Netscape created <strong>SSL</strong>, the U.S. government allowed it to only release a weakened version of it internationally. These controls backfired: it was much easier to acquire the weakened, &quot;international&quot; version, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Export_of_cryptography_from_the_United_States#PC_era" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">so even many Americans used it</a>.</p>
<p>Export controls did not succeed in limiting encryption as the government wished. SSL, PGP, and similar tools were readily available throughout the world, and the controls disadvantaged Americans. Eventually, courts ruled that releasing encryption source code is protected speech, and the U.S. government <a href="https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2019/08/us-export-controls-and-published-encryption-source-code-explained" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">relaxed encryption export controls</a>.</p>
<hr>
<p>Narrowing suppression to &quot;Chinese&quot; models won&#x27;t make things easier. What, exactly, makes an AI model Chinese? Is it Chinese if, as frontier models allege, it was distilled from American models? What about if an American fine-tunes a Chinese model? At best, regulating AI in this way will (temporarily) encumber Americans with red tape and diminished AI access relative to the rest of the world.</p>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="open-source-ai-is-not-just-a-chinese-phenomenon">Open source AI is not just a Chinese phenomenon<a href="#open-source-ai-is-not-just-a-chinese-phenomenon" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to Open source AI is not just a Chinese phenomenon" title="Direct link to Open source AI is not just a Chinese phenomenon" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>There&#x27;s an assumption baked into the open source AI debate that open source models are something that only the Chinese government has an incentive to develop. In reality there are many commercial actors with ample incentive to develop open source AI:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chip makers</strong>: Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang has described what Nvidia is building as “token factories”<sup><a href="#user-content-fn-2-e066d4" id="user-content-fnref-2-e066d4" data-footnote-ref="true" aria-describedby="footnote-label" class="footnoteRefStickyNavbar_i6ta">2</a></sup>. Nvidia doesn&#x27;t care if its chips are used to run frontier models or cheap open source models<sup><a href="#user-content-fn-3-e066d4" id="user-content-fnref-3-e066d4" data-footnote-ref="true" aria-describedby="footnote-label" class="footnoteRefStickyNavbar_i6ta">3</a></sup> - it just wants to produce and generate demand for as many tokens as possible. And indeed Nvidia has itself released a suite of <a href="https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/ai-data-science/foundation-models/nemotron/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">open source models</a>.</li>
<li><strong>American Startups</strong>: Thinking Machines Lab recently released a <a href="https://thinkingmachines.ai/news/introducing-inkling/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">powerful open source model</a>. They and others are betting that models will be commoditized, and a defensible moat can be built around auxiliary services that complement or customize models.</li>
<li><strong>Enterprise AI users</strong>: Frontier model customers aren&#x27;t currently all that active in open source AI development, but they will be. They will want lower-cost models for low-complexity tasks, and more fine-grained control over customer-facing features.</li>
<li><strong>BigCos</strong>: You can be sure that Google and Meta are watching OpenAI&#x27;s new <a href="https://ads.openai.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ad product</a> closely. Should frontier model ad products gain traction, it would be well worth it for these behemoths to commoditize ad-free, open source models to squash ad competition.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="the-ai-race-is-what-exactly">The &quot;AI race&quot; is... what, exactly?<a href="#the-ai-race-is-what-exactly" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to The &quot;AI race&quot; is... what, exactly?" title="Direct link to The &quot;AI race&quot; is... what, exactly?" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>Much of the angst around China&#x27;s models centers on &quot;losing the AI race&quot;. But what&#x27;s the goal of this race? Is it to develop the best model? To sell the most tokens? To destroy humanity first?</p>
<p>Talking about an &quot;AI Race&quot; doesn&#x27;t make more sense than talking about an &quot;Internet Race&quot;. We&#x27;re not competing to be the first to send a rocket to the moon, we&#x27;re reacting to a new, transformational technology. To the extent there&#x27;s a race between nations, it&#x27;s to absorb this transition and grow economies. In this framing, free AI models are a boon, not a threat.</p>
<h2 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="bad-arguments-to-fear-chinese-ai-models">Bad arguments to fear Chinese AI models<a href="#bad-arguments-to-fear-chinese-ai-models" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to Bad arguments to fear Chinese AI models" title="Direct link to Bad arguments to fear Chinese AI models" translate="no">​</a></h2>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="china-is-ai-dumping">China is &quot;AI dumping!&quot;<a href="#china-is-ai-dumping" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to China is &quot;AI dumping!&quot;" title="Direct link to China is &quot;AI dumping!&quot;" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p><a href="https://open.spotify.com/episode/2ulNXWCIYUNx6yE97kmrIC?si=19b6242c57ad49fa" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Scott Galloway has argued</a> that free Chinese AI is an attempt to eliminate competitors in the long run:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>This is what China did to solar panels, steel, EVs, and batteries. First, they match Western quality, or they don&#x27;t even match it. 89%. Close. Actually, match it with cars, they&#x27;ve matched it, but go ahead. Then they cut the price by two thirds, then they own the market.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But apart from chips, AI isn&#x27;t a physical good. Solar panels and steel require physical supply chains, each link of which cannot easily exist on its own. If no one is manufacturing solar panels in your country, it&#x27;s difficult to build a business selling solar-grade silicon wafers.</p>
<p>Software isn&#x27;t like that. An open source model coming from China doesn&#x27;t prevent a fine-tuning business from succeeding in the US - quite the opposite!</p>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="they-will-spread-propaganda">They will spread propaganda!<a href="#they-will-spread-propaganda" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to They will spread propaganda!" title="Direct link to They will spread propaganda!" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>It&#x27;s not unreasonable to assume that Chinese models will be shipped with a pro-China point of view. But this is not a reason to suppress them. <em>The models are open source!</em> If any American has an issue with the political slant of Chinese AI models, they are free to change and release an &quot;Americanized&quot; one. At least within the U.S., it&#x27;s difficult to foresee a model seen as having a distorted pro-China bias outcompeting a substantially similar model with a distorted pro-U.S. bias.</p>
<h3 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="they-will-add-backdoors">They will add backdoors!<a href="#they-will-add-backdoors" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to They will add backdoors!" title="Direct link to They will add backdoors!" translate="no">​</a></h3>
<p>AI does not change the basic market for vulnerabilities: responsible actors patch them, attackers exploit them. Limiting tools for responsible actors only serves attackers.</p>
<p>It&#x27;s theoretically possible for a bad actor to embed hidden adversarial behavior in a model. But if this happens, it serves the interests of responsible actors to find these exploits as soon as possible, and the best way to do this is to let anyone who wants to inspect them.</p>
<h2 class="anchor anchorWithStickyNavbar_LWe7" id="open-source-ai-is-coming">Open source AI is coming<a href="#open-source-ai-is-coming" class="hash-link" aria-label="Direct link to Open source AI is coming" title="Direct link to Open source AI is coming" translate="no">​</a></h2>
<p>It doesn&#x27;t matter much what policy makers or business leaders want: open source AI is too powerful, and too difficult to control. It&#x27;s coming, and attempts to squash it will not amount to anything more than noise along the way.</p>
