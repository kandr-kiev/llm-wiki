                <p>Quick thought experiment: imagine waking up tomorrow and your Gmail account is just... gone. Locked. Some automated system flagged something, there's no human to call, and the recovery form keeps rejecting you.</p>

<p>Sounds dramatic? It happens more often than you'd think. Search "Google locked me out" on any forum and you'll find thousands of posts from people who lost years of email, photos, and — this is the part that should scare you — access to every account that used that email for password resets.</p>

<p>That thought experiment is basically the entire argument for owning your email domain. But it's not the whole story, so let's do this properly.</p>

<h2>
  <a name="the-uncomfortable-truth-about-free-email" href="#the-uncomfortable-truth-about-free-email">
  </a>
  The uncomfortable truth about "free" email
</h2>

<p>Gmail isn't free. You pay with data, attention (ads), and — most importantly — control. Google owns the address, the inbox, and the rules. You're a tenant, and the landlord can change the locks whenever they want.</p>

<p>Most of the time this arrangement is fine. Gmail is genuinely excellent: best-in-class spam filtering, great apps, instant search across fifteen years of email, and it plugs into everything. I'm not going to pretend it isn't a fantastic product. It is.</p>

<p>But there's a difference between using a great product and depending on it for your entire digital identity. Your email address is the skeleton key to your online life. Bank account? Password reset goes to email. Social media? Email. That domain you bought, your cloud storage, your government services login? Email, email, email.</p>

<p>Handing that key to a company with famously nonexistent customer support is a risk most people never consciously accepted — they just slid into it because Gmail was the default.</p>

<h2>
  <a name="what-youre-actually-buying-with-a-personal-domain" href="#what-youre-actually-buying-with-a-personal-domain">
  </a>
  What you're actually buying with a personal domain
</h2>

<p>When you register yourname.com (or .me, or whatever fits), you're not buying email. You're buying three things:</p>

<p><strong>Portability</strong><br>
Your address stops being tied to any provider. Using Fastmail today, annoyed with them tomorrow? Move your domain to Proton, Migadu, Zoho, wherever. Your address stays <a href="mailto:you@yourname.com">you@yourname.com</a> forever. Nobody you correspond with ever knows anything changed. Try doing that with Gmail — you can't. Leaving Gmail means getting a new identity and telling literally everyone.</p>

<p><strong>Control over spam, for real</strong><br>
Catch-all addresses are the underrated superpower here. You can hand out a unique address to every service — amazon@, unitedairlines@, thatweirdforum@ — all landing in one inbox. The day one of them starts getting junk, you know exactly who leaked or sold your data, and you can nuke just that address. You go from "spam victim" to "spam detective," and honestly it's a little addictive.</p>

<p><strong>A signal</strong><br>
Like it or not, <a href="mailto:you@yourname.com">you@yourname.com</a> reads differently than <a href="mailto:yourname84@gmail.com">yourname84@gmail.com</a> — to clients, employers, and anyone you email cold. It's the digital version of a firm handshake. Small thing, real effect.</p>

<h2>
  <a name="now-the-honest-downsides" href="#now-the-honest-downsides">
  </a>
  Now the honest downsides
</h2>

<p>I'd be doing you dirty if I only told you the romantic version. So:</p>

<p><strong>It's a subscription to your own identity</strong><br>
Domains cost around 10–15 USD a year, hosting another 30–60 USD depending on the provider. Not expensive, but it's forever. Stop paying and you don't just lose email — you risk someone else registering your expired domain and receiving your password resets. That's the single scariest failure mode of this whole setup, and it doesn't exist with Gmail. Auto-renew isn't optional. It's the whole ballgame.</p>

<p><strong>You inherit a chore list</strong><br>
SPF, DKIM, DMARC — the DNS alphabet soup that tells the world your mail is legit. Get it wrong and your messages quietly land in other people's spam folders, which is worse than having no email at all. Good providers walk you through it these days, but it's still an afternoon of fiddling that Gmail simply never asks of you.</p>

<p><strong>Migration is a slow grind</strong><br>
You'll be finding accounts tied to your old address for months. That store you ordered from once in 2021? Yep, still emailing your Gmail. It's death by a thousand login screens.</p>

<p><strong>The Google ecosystem tax</strong><br>
"Sign in with Google," calendar invites that just work, Drive sharing, YouTube, Maps timeline. You can keep a Gmail account for all that, of course — but then you're maintaining two inboxes, which is its own small tax.</p>

<h2>
  <a name="if-you-do-make-the-jump-do-it-right" href="#if-you-do-make-the-jump-do-it-right">
  </a>
  If you do make the jump, do it right
</h2>

<ol>
<li>Register the domain at a boring, reliable registrar — Cloudflare or Porkbun, not some random discount site.</li>
<li>Enable auto-renew on day one. Add a calendar reminder a month before expiry anyway.</li>
<li>Pick a provider that automates the DNS setup — Fastmail, Proton, and Purelymail all basically hold your hand.</li>
<li>Don't rush. Start using the new address for new things, then deliberately migrate the critical accounts: banking, government, work, main logins.</li>
<li>Keep the Gmail account alive and forwarding. It's your safety net, and it costs nothing to keep.</li>
</ol>

<h2>
  <a name="bottom-line" href="#bottom-line">
  </a>
  Bottom line
</h2>

<p>Gmail is a great product that happens to own you a little. A <a href="https://timotic.me" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">personal domain</a> is a small yearly bill that buys you independence, portability, and a master key that's actually yours.<br>
Neither choice is wrong. But one of them is a choice — and the other is just the default you never questioned.<br>
Ten bucks a year to own your name on the internet. For me, that's the easiest money I spend.</p>
